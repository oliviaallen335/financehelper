Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)
Analysis
DCF Analysis is a broad, inclusive term largely for:
- Preparation of cash flow estimates of a project
- Use of those estimates in capital budgeting analysis
- DCF is a part of the NPV calculation

Cash Flow Not Profit
Internal investment projects are attractive due to the cash flow they generate
- A firm can spend its operating cash flow but not its net
- income
- Accounting income (net income) includes many accrued and deferred items and may lead to incorrect decisions

Incremental Cash Flow
When conducting DCF analysis, you must discount the incremental free cash flows of a project
- Find the difference between the expected cash flows with a project minus the cash flows without the project
- Accounts for only the incremental (marginal) cash flows from an investment and not ones that would already occur
- Best to think about them in categories

Incremental Cash Flow
1. Include all indirect effects
- Trace out any indirect impact on future cash flows
- Cannibalization/Erosion costs: costs that arise when a new product or service competes with revenue generated by a current product or service
- Synergy gains: increased revenues for other existing products related to the introduction of a new product

Incremental Cash Flow
2. Ignore sunk costs
- Expenses that have already been incurred, or that will be incurred, regardless of the decision to accept or reject a project
- Should not be considered as incremental cash flow
3. Include opportunity cost
- Benefit or cash flow forgone as a result of an action
- Compare “with or without” not “before versus after”
- Examples: idle land or equipment
- Should be included as incremental cash flow

Incremental Cash Flow
4. Recognize the investment in working capital
- Net working capital is current assets minus current liabilities
- Investment in current assets: inventories and accounts receivables
- Investment in current liabilities: accounts payable and other accruals
- Note: investments in working capital can change throughout a projects life and are recovered at the end of the project

Incremental Cash Flow
5. Remember terminal cash flows
- Recovery of investments in working capital
- Disposal costs (both positive and negative)
6. Beware of allocated overhead costs
- Costs that cannot conveniently be charged to any specific cost center (project) but are associated with running the business
- Accounts allocate these to projects based on overhead rates

Investment vs Financing
Typically, you ignore how a project is financed when valuing it as an investment
- Separating investing and financing decisions
- Treat as all equity financed with all cash inflows going to shareholders (interest payments on debt not included in incremental cash flow)
- Ask the question: Is this project’s existence dependent on financing?

Forecasting Cash Flows
This is not meant to be an equation but rather a guide to think about measuring and including cash flows in a DCF analysis

Operating Cash Flow
OCF is what we generally care about when forecasting incremental cash flows
- Method 1: Dollars in, dollars out:

OCF = revenues – expenses – taxes
- Method 2: Adjusted accounting profit

OCF = NOPAT + depreciation
- Method 3: Depreciation tax shield

OCF = (revenues – cash expenses) x (1 – tax rate) + (tax rate x depreciation)

Capital Investment
Capital expenditure generally involves an initial cash outflow, but attention should also be paid to the disposal of the capital equipment
- Disposal cost could be a cash outflow (true costs, cleaning, etc) or a cash inflow (salvage value)
- After-tax Salvage Value = Selling Price – Tax rate * (Selling price –
Book Value)

The manager of the Grand Junction Furniture Company decides to dispose of the specialty tools, acquired 2 years ago at a cost of $430,000 (including installation), to another firm for $125,000. How much of an after-tax cash flow will result, assuming that the tools were being depreciated based on a 3-year straight-line depreciation and the company’s marginal tax rate is

Depreciation
There are several ways a firm can handle depreciation:
1. Straight-line Depreciation:

Fixed depreciation expense over the life of an asset

Typically equals cost divided usable life
2. Double-declining / MACRS

Depreciation expense declines over life of asset

Typically straight-line expense x 2 per year
3. “Bonus” depreciation

Allows for 100% depreciation expense i

Investment in Working Capital
Net working capital is the difference between current assets and current liabilities
- Increases in NWC typically occur at the beginning (increase in inventory and accounts receivables) = investment (cash outflow)
- Investment in NWC usually offset at end of project’s life = payout (cash inflow)
- Make sure to remember the cash flow is the CHANGE in NWC
